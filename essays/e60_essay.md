One of the most popular aspirations for software engineers is to start one's own company. Many of us, and me in particular, have the origins of big companies ingrained into our minds. I remember being amazed at the rags-to-riches tales, where undergrads or college dropouts creating a startup in their own home, which later grows into a global, million-dollar company. However, I also dread the software engineer's dream at the same time. The stories of big tech exploiting its user base for profit are becoming more common, and a part of me fears that my future company could transform into a selfish, apathetic institution. In an attempt to trace the path of FANG (Facebook, Amazon, Netflix, Google), I took an entrepreneurial class to learn about creating and maintaining a startup, and what I've learned revealed a nasty truth about the internal working of a company.
 
 There are many examples of companies that become an apathetic corporation despite its humble and altruistic beginning. For example, a company like Google is infamous in the news. The slogan: "don't be evil" is starting to fade away within the culture of the corporation, after rises of privacy violations and data collection begins to surface. As early as 2007, Google collected information on unencrypted WIFI networks in its data collection for Google Maps. In my time in the entrepreneurial class, most big tech seems to follow this trend, where the company strays from its initial promises of creating public good for one reason or another. There are many causes for such transformation, but the main reason that I believe to be the root cause is the disconnect from the public. Startups are focused on growing, and gaining new users, and rolling out new features, and hiring engineers, and marketing their product, and more, and more, and more ...
 
 
 ... and completely ignoring how their company and internal culture is changing. Every resource and manpower is focused on keeping the company growing, that many employees in the company left the thoughts of maintaining a good-moral standing, sustainable business practice. It seemed impossible to stop this transformation from happening. The only thing on everyone's mind is to grow, and the bigger the company grows, the less amount of thought is put into the consequences of their actions. 
 
What keeps us grounded in reality, even if our companies are becoming larger than life, is adhering to a code of professional ethics. The ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct, IEEE Computer Society Code of Ethics, or even something as simple as Thomas Hobbes' state of nature allows big tech companies to keep the consequences of their actions in mind. In them, the code requires us to consider how our code could impact the public, it asks us to give it our best effort to provide our users with the most stable, best quality possible. These requirements would interfere with the startup ideology of growth, but they retain the company's humanity as well.
